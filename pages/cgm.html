The Conventional Gait Model

Introduction

The name Conventional Gait Model refers to a number of very similar and inter-related biomechanical models developed for clinical gait analysis in the 1980s. One variant was developed for internal use in the Newington Children’s Hospital (now the Connecticut Children’s Medical Centre) and is known as the Newington, Davis or Gage models. Another was developed at the Helen Hayes Hospital in New York and was made available for use in 6 other centres and is known as the Helen Hayes or Kadaba model (sometimes as the Helen Hayes Markerset). Vicon worked with staff in both centres when developing there Clinical Manager software and this became known as the VCM model. This was later cloned as Plug-in Gait (PiG) for Workstation software. Most current manufacturers of clinical gait analysis systems have now produce some version of the model.

The model has been described in a number of papers:

  • Kadaba, M. P., Ramakrishnan, H. K., & Wootten, M. E. (1990). Measurement of lower extremity kinematics during level walking. Journal of Orthopaedic Research, 8(3), 383-392.
  • Kadaba, M. P., Ramakrishnan, H. K., Wootten, M. E., Gainey, J., Gorton, G., & Cochran, G. V. (1989). Repeatability of kinematic, kinetic, and electromyographic data in normal adult gait. Journal of Orthopaedic Research, 7(6), 849-860.
  • Ramakrishnan, H. K., & Kadaba, M. P. (1991). On the estimation of joint kinematics during gait. Journal of Biomechanics, 24(10), 969-977.
  • Davis, R. B., Ounpuu, S., Tyburski, D., & Gage, J. (1991). A gait analysis data collection and reduction technique. Human Movement Science, 10, 575-587.
  • Ounpuu, S., Gage, J., & Davis, R. (1991). Three-dimensional lower extremity joint kinetics in normal pediatric gait. Journal of Pediatric Orthopaedics, 11, 341-349.
There has always been considerable confusion as to how the models are related with a general perception that the models are quite different. They are actually extremely similar. The term Conventional Gait Model was thus coined to emphasize their essential similarity. It was first used by Richard Baker when presenting a tutorial, All you ever wanted to know about the Conventional Gait model but were afraid to ask, to the Gait and Clinical Movement Analysis Society in 2003.
